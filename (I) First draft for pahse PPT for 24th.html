01 — Introduction
The Problem We Are Solving
Why this project exists — moving from intuition-based talent decisions to data-driven performance prediction
❌
No Standard Success Profile
No defined benchmark for what 'excellent' looks like in HR Manager and HRBP roles. Hiring and promotion decisions lack a competency-based foundation.
📉
Inconsistent Performance Ratings
Ratings vary across managers and departments with no structured basis. Performance scores are lagging indicators, not leading predictors.
🎯
Reactive Talent Decisions
No data-backed tool to proactively identify at-risk or high-potential employees. Talent decisions happen after problems emerge, not before.
💡
The Objective
Move from intuition-based talent decisions to data-driven performance prediction — by identifying the competencies that actually differentiate top performers from average ones, and building a model that can predict success before it happens.
02 — Business Case
Why This Project Matters
Strategic value for Jio Platforms — how success profile analytics transforms HR decision-making
📈 Talent Retention ROI
Our exit interview analysis shows 58% leave for compensation (market pull), but 18% leave for growth/career reasons — the only segment where HR intervention actually works. A success profile lets us proactively design growth paths for high-potential employees before they consider leaving.
🎯 Precision in Hiring
Currently, hiring decisions rely on interviews and manager judgement alone. With validated competency profiles, we can assess candidates against the same behavioral standards that our top performers exhibit — reducing mis-hires and improving time-to-productivity.
⚡ Development Focus
Learning & Development budgets are limited. Our stay interview data shows only ~10% are active learners. By knowing which competencies predict success, L&D can target the highest-ROI development interventions for each employee tier.
🔄 Succession Planning
With 920 employees analysed and 184 top performers identified, we now have an evidence-based pipeline for succession. The competency model becomes the common language for "ready for next level" discussions.
Bharat's Direction (June 12 Meeting)
"The project should be positioned as a Success Profile Analytics framework rather than a rating-only prediction model. The final model should explain what makes employees successful in a particular role by combining quantitative performance data with qualitative behavioural and managerial insights."

05 — Methodology: Step 1
Rating Encoding & Feature Engineering
How we converted raw performance text into computable numeric scores — then built 4 derived metrics
Rating Encoding Map
RatingNumericMeaning
A*4Exceptional
A+3Exceeds expectations
A2Meets expectations
B1Below expectations
Text strings like "A*" cannot be averaged or trended. Once converted to numbers, we can calculate 5-year averages, detect improvement/decline, measure consistency, and apply recency weights.
4 Engineered Features
1
avg_rating — Overall Level
Mean across all 5 years. Proxy for sustained performance quality.
2
rating_trend — Trajectory
RTG25-26 minus RTG21-22. Positive = improving.
3
rating_consistency — Reliability
Std dev across years. Low = reliable, consistent performer.
4
recency_weighted_rtg — Current State
Weighted avg: recent years matter more (10/15/20/25/30%).

07 — Survey Design
All-Employee Behavioral Survey
Multi-format, multi-section survey designed to capture quantitative scores, belief mapping, scenarios, and qualitative narratives
A
Section A: Behavioral Frequency Scale (40 items) ▼
40 items across 10 competency domains, rated on a 5-point frequency scale (1=Rarely to 5=Almost Always). Each domain has 4 behavioral statements. Generates quantitative per-item scores for statistical comparison between top and average performers.
Execution Excellence Strategic Thinking Learning Agility Collaboration Problem Solving Drive & Initiative People Leadership Communication Resilience Customer Orientation
B
Section B: Success Belief Mapping (10 items) ▼
Respondents rate how important each competency is for success in their role (1=Not Important to 5=Critical), plus forced ranking of top 3. This reveals the mental model of success — top performers may weight competencies differently than average performers, and that gap is itself a predictive signal.
C
Section C: Behavioral Scenarios (8 scenarios) ▼
8 realistic workplace scenarios with 4 answer options each. No right or wrong answers — choice patterns between top vs. average performers reveal specific behavioral tendencies. This bypasses social desirability bias present in self-rating scales.
D
Section D: Open-Ended Reflections (5 questions) ▼
Richest qualitative source. Covers achievements, challenges, self-differentiation, growth gaps, and advice to new colleagues. Responses are coded for competency themes — top performer narrative patterns directly become STAR interview question stems.
E
Section E: Contextual Demographics (6 items) ▼
Function, grade, tenure, team size, cross-functional frequency. Enables statistical controls — ensuring like-for-like comparison between top and average performers at the same grade and function.
💡 Core Design Principle: Each competency is measured in at least two ways (frequency + scenario or frequency + open-ended), improving reliability and reducing bias.

09 — Challenges
Challenges We Are Facing
Current blockers and constraints in building the behavioral competency framework
🔒 Limited Historical Competency Data from HR
It is significantly more difficult to design a precise, validated behavioral questionnaire without access to the organization's historical competency data. Past performance reviews, prior assessment tools, and existing competency frameworks would provide the baseline behavioral language and validated indicators specific to Jio's culture. Without this historical reference, we are building the survey instrument from external frameworks (Lominger 67, SHL OPQ) and must validate relevance through interviews before survey deployment.
Data Access
🔍 Scarce Public Research on Performance Predictor Models
Companies do not publish their performance predictor models on the internet. Academic literature covers competency theory extensively, but organization-specific implementation details — especially the correlation between behavioral survey scores and actual performance ratings — are treated as proprietary. This makes it difficult to find benchmark validation studies that compare survey-derived competency scores against 5-year OLP performance data. Our model must break new ground in this space.
Research Gap
🧠 Understanding the Behavioral Side of Employees
Behavioral data is inherently more complex and subjective than quantitative performance ratings. Designing questions that surface authentic behaviors — not socially desirable responses — requires careful scenario construction, multi-format validation, and pilot testing. The 18–30 month tenure window has been identified as the critical attrition trigger, yet understanding the behavioral patterns that lead to staying vs. leaving requires qualitative depth that quantitative OLP data alone cannot provide.
Behavioral Complexity
✅ Mitigation: Multi-Source Triangulation Design
To address these challenges, we have designed a 3-source triangulation method — only competencies confirmed by 2 or more independent sources (Top Performer interviews + Average Performer interviews + Manager interviews) will enter the final model. This compensates for the lack of historical data by generating primary behavioral evidence directly from the current workforce. Inter-rater reliability target: ≥0.80 (Cohen's kappa).

11 — KPIs & Success Metrics
How We'll Measure Success
Key performance indicators and validation criteria for the final deliverables
📊
Model Accuracy
Competency survey scores must correlate with OLP composite scores at r ≥ 0.50 for the model to have predictive validity.
🎯
Survey Response Rate
Target ≥60% response rate across all functions. Below 50% triggers alternative distribution channels.
✅
Inter-Rater Reliability
Two independent coders must achieve Cohen's kappa ≥ 0.80 on 20% of interview transcripts.
🔬
Statistical Significance
Mann-Whitney U test: p < 0.05 AND effect size r > 0.30 for competency items to qualify as differentiators.
📈
Cronbach's Alpha
Internal consistency of each competency domain must exceed α > 0.70 for survey validation.
🗣️
Interview Coverage
Minimum 13–18 interviews completed (10–15 top performers + 5 average contrast group) across ≥3 functions.
