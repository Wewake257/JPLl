1. Executive Summary

📍 Current Status — Step 2: Conduct Competency Survey
We have successfully completed Step 1: Identify Top / Avg / Low Performers using OLP composite scores. We are now actively executing Step 2: Conduct Competency Survey — deploying the level‑adapted survey instrument to collect behavioural data from 150 employees.

Steps 3–10 (Extract Key Competencies, Map to Lominger, Build Interviews, Assign Weights, Validate, Build Matrix, Test, and Apply) are all upcoming.

This document serves as the internal design specification and methodology reference for the Phase 1 Competency Survey — the second step in the 10‑step end‑to‑end process for building a predictive performance model at Jio Platforms Limited.

The project was initiated due to a critical gap: the organisation lacks a validated, context‑specific competency model that can predict employee performance for HR Manager and HRBP roles in the JioCX business unit. Five years of OLP performance ratings exist for 920 employees, enabling identification of top performers by quantitative composite score. However, no data exists on the specific behaviours and competencies that explain why those employees outperform their peers.

We are now at Step 2: deploying a rigorous, level‑adapted competency survey to a target cohort of 150 employees. The survey instrument employs a mixed‑method design combining:

  • 20 Likert items (self‑perceived behavioural frequency on a 1–5 scale), distributed across 10 competencies (2 items each), with 1 reverse‑scored item per competency (50% reverse‑scored) to mitigate acquiescence and straight‑lining.
  • 2 attention checks embedded within the Likert section to identify inattentive or satisficing respondents.
  • Forced ranking (1–10) of the 10 competencies, measuring the mental model of what drives success, with a Top‑3 cross‑check to validate reflective ranking.
  • 3 scenario‑based ranking exercises per job level (Junior/Manager/Leader), each requiring respondents to rank 4 behavioural options from most to least effective – revealing default behavioural autopilot.
  • 5 open‑ended narrative questions with a 40–50 word minimum, measuring agency, attribution style, and generating interview language for Phase 1.
  • 4 contextual demographic controls (job level, work hours, direct reports, cross‑functional involvement).

Critical design decision: Each job level (Junior, Manager, Leader) receives a fully level‑adapted questionnaire — the Likert items, scenarios, and belief items are all tailored to the respondent's seniority and scope of work. This ensures fair comparison within levels and meaningful differentiation across levels.

The survey is deployed as a self‑contained HTML file (or can be adapted to Microsoft Forms) with responses collected centrally for analysis. This HTML document is the internal design specification — it contains the complete methodology, item content, and level‑adaptation logic that has been implemented in the survey.

3. Survey Architecture Overview

3.1 Multi‑Method Design Philosophy

The survey employs four different response formats to capture complementary types of data about the same underlying behavioural constructs. This redundancy serves two purposes: (a) it increases the reliability of measurement for each competency by triangulating across multiple methods, and (b) it provides built‑in cross‑validation, making it possible to detect low‑quality, socially desirable, or inconsistent responses.

FormatPurposeBias MitigationPrimary Bias Risk
Likert Scale (Section A) Self‑perceived behavioural frequency across 20 items (2 per competency) Provides scaled baseline; partially mitigated by Section B and C cross‑checks Central tendency, acquiescence bias
Forced Ranking (Section B) Mental model of what drives success – eliminates ‘All 5s’ problem Cannot be gamed with uniform responses; must produce unique distribution Straight‑lining, central tendency
Scenario Ranking (Section C) Default behavioural autopilot under realistic situational pressure No single obviously ‘correct’ answer; bypasses most social desirability Framing bias (mitigated by neutral language)
Open‑Ended Narratives (Section D) Agency, attribution style, and authentic language for interview design Reveals genuine mental models; provides coding corpus for Phase 1 Social desirability (mitigated by verb‑density coding)

5. Theoretical & Empirical Foundations

Why these frameworks and not others? The survey is not constructed in a theoretical vacuum. Its architecture is explicitly derived from peer‑reviewed frameworks in industrial‑organisational (I‑O) psychology. The selection was guided by three criteria: (1) empirical evidence of predictive validity for job performance, (2) direct translatability into observable behavioural items, and (3) cost‑effective and non‑proprietary implementation within an internship timeline. Below we detail the foundational research that anchors this survey, followed by a transparent rationale for excluding alternative frameworks.

🧠 Core Thesis: Performance is best predicted by measuring behavioural frequency (Likert), implicit priorities (ranking), situational judgement (scenarios), and narrative reasoning (open‑ended) – a multi‑method approach validated by over five decades of I‑O psychology research.

5.1 Core Foundational Frameworks (Included)

Framework / ResearchKey ContributionHow It Is Operationalised in This Survey
Campbell’s Model of Job Performance (1990) Performance is multi‑dimensional: Task, Contextual, and Adaptive performance. Our 10 competencies are explicitly mapped to these three pillars (see Section 4.2). Ensures comprehensive coverage of the performance domain.
Bartram’s “Great Eight” Competency Model (2005) Eight competencies universally predict performance across roles and industries. Our 10 competencies are a curated expansion of the Great Eight, tailored to JioCX’s specific managerial/HRBP context. Provides the nomological net for item generation.
Lominger / Korn Ferry Leadership Architect Industry‑standard competency library used in talent management. Step 3 of our process explicitly maps extracted competencies to the Lominger framework for organisational alignment and standardisation.
Classical Test Theory (CTT) – Cronbach (1951), Nunnally (1978) Provides the psychometric foundation for reliability (Cronbach’s α) and validity assessment. Guides our data cleaning, item analysis, and reliability testing (Section 9 & 10). Ensures the survey produces internally consistent and stable scores.
Ajzen’s Theory of Planned Behavior (1991) Behavioural intention is the immediate antecedent of actual behaviour. Section C (Situational Judgement) measures behavioural intentions under realistic work dilemmas, bypassing social desirability to tap into true default responses.
Motowidlo et al. (1997) – SJT Validity Situational Judgement Tests (SJTs) predict job performance above and beyond cognitive ability and personality. Section C scenarios are designed as low‑fidelity SJTs. They reveal tacit knowledge and practical intelligence, which are critical for managerial and HRBP roles.
Dweck’s Mindset Theory (2006) Growth mindset (belief that abilities can be developed) correlates with higher performance and resilience. Section D open‑ended questions (e.g., “Area for Growth”) are coded for growth‑oriented language, providing a behavioural proxy for mindset.
Bandura’s Self‑Efficacy Theory (1977) Belief in one’s capability to execute behaviours influences performance outcomes. Section A Likert items measure self‑perceived behavioural frequency – a direct operationalisation of domain‑specific self‑efficacy at work.

5.2 Rationale for Exclusion (Why Not Others)

Several well‑known frameworks were deliberately excluded. The table below provides the specific justification for each exclusion, based on theoretical misalignment, practical constraints, or lack of evidence for job performance prediction.

Excluded FrameworkReason for ExclusionJustification
Big Five / OCEAN (McCrae & Costa, 1987) Too trait‑based and dispositional; weak incremental validity for specific job behaviours. Big Five measures broad, stable personality traits (e.g., Extraversion). These are poor predictors of specific on‑the‑job behaviours because they do not capture context‑dependent skills. Meta‑analyses (e.g., Barrick & Mount, 1991) show corrected correlations of only r = 0.10–0.25 with job performance – insufficient for our predictive modelling goals. Furthermore, traits are difficult to translate into actionable development feedback.
MBTI (Myers‑Briggs Type Indicator) Poor predictive validity; categorises rather than measures; not evidence‑based for performance. MBTI has been widely criticised in I‑O psychology for its lack of predictive validity for job performance (see Pittenger, 2005). Its dichotomous (categorical) nature forces individuals into boxes, ignoring the dimensional nature of behaviour. It is not designed for performance prediction and has no place in a competency‑based assessment system.
DiSC Profile Focuses on communication and interaction styles, not performance competencies. DiSC is useful for team building and understanding interpersonal dynamics, but it does not measure task execution, problem‑solving, or strategic thinking – all of which are critical for HR Manager/HRBP roles. It lacks the breadth needed for a comprehensive performance model.
Hogan Assessment Systems (HPI, HDS, MVPI) Proprietary, expensive, and requires certified practitioners. Hogan is a well‑validated tool, but it is a commercial product. The cost and certification barrier make it unfeasible for an internship project with a limited budget. Additionally, it focuses on personality and dark‑side traits, whereas our project requires a purely competency‑based, behavioural model for internal calibration.
McKinsey 7S Framework Macro‑organisational strategy framework; not designed for individual‑level performance. McKinsey 7S operates at the level of the organisation (Strategy, Structure, Systems, etc.). It does not provide a taxonomy of individual behaviours or competencies. Applying it to individual performance prediction would be a category error.
Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton) Designed for organisational performance management, not individual prediction. The Balanced Scorecard translates organisational strategy into performance metrics (Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth). It is a strategy execution tool, not a behavioural competency model. It cannot differentiate between high and low performers at the individual level.
Iceberg Model of Competency (Spencer & Spencer, 1993) Too abstract and static; difficult to operationalise into survey items. While conceptually influential, the Iceberg Model divides competencies into “visible” (skills/knowledge) and “hidden” (self‑concept, traits, motives) layers. The hidden layer is exceptionally difficult to measure reliably via self‑report survey without psychometric validation (e.g., projective tests). Our chosen frameworks focus on measurable behaviours (Campbell, Bartram) rather than unobservable motives.
Gallup CliftonStrengths Focuses on strengths (talents) rather than performance behaviours. CliftonStrengths identifies innate talent themes (e.g., Achiever, Strategic). While useful for career development, it does not measure the frequency or quality of behaviour execution. Performance depends on how talents are applied, not just their presence. Our survey measures applied behaviour, not inherent talent.
🔬 Why the Chosen Frameworks Work Together:
  • Campbell + Bartram provide the content (what to measure).
  • Ajzen + Motowidlo provide the format (how to measure it – via intentions and judgement).
  • CTT + Bandura provide the psychometric rigour (reliability, self‑efficacy calibration).
  • Dweck provides the qualitative lens (narrative coding for growth mindset).

This multi‑layered foundation ensures that the survey is not merely a collection of questions, but a theoretically grounded, empirically defensible instrument tailored specifically to predict performance in the JioCX context.

7. Item Design Methodology

7.1 Section A — Likert Scale (20 Items)

Format: 1–5 Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree). Each competency has 2 items.

Reverse‑Scored Items (50%): Exactly 1 item per competency is reverse‑scored, totalling 10 reverse‑scored items. This high proportion is intentional to minimise acquiescence bias and straight‑lining.

Attention Checks: 2 checks embedded at positions 7 and 14 after shuffling (AC1: select "Agree" (4); AC2: select "Strongly Disagree" (1)).

Shuffling: Items are mixed across competency groups and shuffled on each load.

7.2 Section B — Forced Ranking (1–10) + Top‑3 Cross‑Check

Respondents rank the 10 competencies from 1 (most critical) to 10 (least critical). No ties permitted. Used ranks are hidden from other dropdowns. A Top‑3 cross‑check validates the reflective ranking.

7.3 Section C — Scenario‑Based Ranking

Three scenarios per level. Each scenario has 4 options to rank from 1 (most effective) to 4 (least effective). Used ranks (1–4) are hidden within each scenario.

7.4 Section D — Open‑Ended Reflections

Five questions with a 40–50 word minimum: D1 (Professional Achievement), D2 (Navigating Ambiguity), D3 (Your Distinct Value), D4 (Area for Growth), D5 (What It Takes to Excel Here).

7.5 Section E — Contextual Demographics

Job Level, Average Weekly Work Hours, Number of Direct Reports, Cross‑Functional Project Involvement. Name and Employee ID are also collected for OLP merge.

FilterRule
Incomplete Response Exclude any respondent completing fewer than 80% of all 44 fields.
Straight‑Liner Flag For Section A, calculate standard deviation per respondent across all 20 items. Exclude SD < 0.5.
Attention Check Fail Exclude any respondent who fails either of the two embedded attention checks (AC1 or AC2).
Rank Violation Exclude any Section B or Section C respondent who did not use each unique rank exactly once.
Speed Filter Exclude responses completed in under 10 minutes (expected completion ~25–30 min).
Section B–C Consistency Flag respondents whose Section B top‑ranked competency does not appear in their Top‑3 cross‑check – manual review.

13. Limitations

⚠️ Key Limitations to Acknowledge
  1. Common Method Variance (CMV): All data collected in this survey is self‑reported. Without external validation (manager ratings, peer feedback, or objective behavioural observation), we cannot definitively separate self‑perception from actual competency. This limitation is partially addressed by the multi‑method design (Likert + forced ranking + scenarios + open‑ended), but CMV remains the primary methodological concern.
  2. Cross‑Sectional Design: The survey captures a single behavioural snapshot. Performance is dynamic; the same individual may display different competency profiles under different organisational conditions (e.g., during high‑pressure periods vs. stable periods).
  3. Platform Constraints: If deployed via Microsoft Forms, it does not natively support Semantic Differential bipolar format or drag‑and‑drop ranking. The survey uses a Likert scale and dropdown menus, which may reduce the discriminating power of some items compared to a native Qualtrics or HTML prototype.
  4. Response Rate Risk: With a target of 150 complete responses, there is a risk of low participation. If response rates are uneven across job levels (e.g., leaders respond less than juniors), the competency model will have unequal reliability across the hierarchy.
  5. Social Desirability Bias: Despite mitigation strategies, respondents may still inflate their self‑ratings, particularly in Section A (Likert) and Section D (open‑ended). The forced ranking and scenario sections are designed to bypass this, but they cannot eliminate it entirely.
  6. Dependency on OLP Data: The validity of the model depends on the accuracy and completeness of the OLP composite scores used to identify top performers. Any errors in the OLP dataset will cascade into the competency model.
  7. Sample Representativeness: The survey is limited to the JioCX business unit. Generalising findings to other business units or to the wider Jio organisation may not be valid without additional validation.

14. Future Recommendations

🚀 Recommendations for Subsequent Phases and Future Iterations
  1. Transition to Qualtrics for Phase 2/3: Qualtrics natively supports Semantic Differential, drag‑and‑drop ranking, and advanced conditional logic. The improved format fidelity alone is expected to meaningfully increase the discriminating power of Section A‑equivalent items.
  2. Implement 360‑Degree Component: In future survey cycles, incorporate a manager‑rating parallel survey where direct managers rate their reports on the same competencies. This eliminates Common Method Variance and adds an external validation layer that self‑report alone cannot provide.
  3. Add Forced‑Choice Pairwise Format: Replace the Likert scale with pairwise behavioural comparisons (e.g., "Which is more like you: A or B?"). This is the methodological gold standard for eliminating faking and straight‑lining in competency surveys.
  4. Longitudinal Tracking: Link the final competency model to actual performance outcomes (OLP composite scores, promotion rates, attrition flags) over an 18‑month horizon. This transitions the model from cross‑sectional to truly predictive and allows causality to be assessed.
  5. NLP‑Assisted Coding for Open‑Ended Responses: For future survey cycles, leverage Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools to automate thematic coding of Section D responses. This enables near‑real‑time analysis and removes the manual effort bottleneck from the analytical pipeline.
  6. Expand to Other Business Units: Once validated within JioCX, the competency model should be tested and calibrated across other Jio business units (e.g., Retail, Digital, Cloud) to ensure organisation‑wide applicability.
  7. Integrate with Learning & Development: Use the validated competency model to design targeted L&D interventions. For example, if "Strategic Thinking" (ST) is identified as a key differentiator, create a dedicated programme to develop ST skills among high‑potential junior employees.
