1. Executive Summary Why This Review

This document provides a comprehensive academic and empirical review of the five foundational frameworks that underpin the JioCX Competency Survey and Performance Predictor project. It answers:

  • What is the theoretical origin and empirical evidence for each framework?
  • Who are the seminal researchers and what are the key publications?
  • How does each framework directly inform the survey design and items?
  • Why these frameworks are the most appropriate choice for this project.
🎯 Core Thesis: The survey integrates five complementary I‑O Psychology frameworks — Iceberg (Spencer & Spencer, 1993), Lominger (Lombardo & Eichinger, 1996), SHL Great Eight (Bartram, 2005), Campbell’s Performance Model (1990), and Classical Test Theory (Cronbach, 1951; Nunnally, 1978) — to create a psychometrically robust, theoretically grounded, and practically actionable competency measurement instrument.

2. Framework 1: The Iceberg Model Spencer & Spencer (1993)

📖 Academic Origin

Source: Spencer, L. M., & Spencer, S. M. (1993). Competence at Work: Models for Superior Performance. John Wiley & Sons.

Precursor: The model builds on McClelland’s (1973) seminal paper “Testing for Competence Rather Than for ‘Intelligence’” (American Psychologist, Vol. 28, pp. 1‑14), which argued that traditional aptitude and intelligence tests are poor predictors of job performance, and that competencies (behavioural characteristics) are far more predictive.

Core Premise: Competencies are organised like an iceberg:

  • Visible (above water): Skills & Knowledge — easy to observe and develop.
  • Hidden (below water): Self‑Concept, Traits, and Motives — the real drivers of long‑term performance.

Empirical Evidence: Spencer & Spencer analysed competency models from over 200 jobs across 400+ organisations. They found that hidden competencies (particularly achievement motive and influence) consistently distinguished superior performers from average performers, with effect sizes (d) ranging from 0.40 to 0.80.

🧊 How It Operates in This Survey
  • Section A (Likert): Measures visible behavioural frequency (skills in action).
  • Section C (Scenarios): Taps into hidden tacit knowledge and behavioural intentions.
  • Section D (Open‑ended): Codes for hidden motives (achievement, affiliation) and attribution style.
  • Section B (Ranking): Reveals the hidden implicit mental model of what drives success.
📌 Key Reference
McClelland, D. C. (1973). Testing for competence rather than for "intelligence." American Psychologist, 28(1), 1–14.

Meta‑analytic evidence: Spencer & Spencer (1993) reported that achievement motive correlates r = 0.25–0.40 with managerial performance across multiple studies.

3. Framework 2: Lominger Leadership Architect Lombardo & Eichinger (1996)

📖 Academic Origin

Source: Lombardo, M. M., & Eichinger, R. W. (1996). The Leadership Architect. Lominger Limited. (Now published by Korn Ferry as the Korn Ferry Leadership Architect).

Core Premise: The framework provides a standardised library of 67 competencies, each defined with specific behavioural anchors, development suggestions, and interview probes. It was built from decades of research on what differentiates superior leaders from average ones.

Research Base: Lominger was developed using a combination of:

  • In‑depth interviews with over 20,000 managers across industries.
  • 360‑degree feedback data from over 100,000 leaders.
  • Correlational studies linking competencies to business unit performance outcomes.

Empirical Evidence: Korn Ferry (2019) reported that the Lominger framework has a criterion‑related validity of r = 0.34–0.48 with managerial effectiveness ratings, and r = 0.29–0.41 with business performance metrics (e.g., revenue growth).

📋 How It Operates in This Survey
  • All 10 competencies are mapped directly to Lominger competency definitions (see mapping table in Section 6).
  • Behavioural anchors for Likert items are derived from Lominger’s “proficiency levels”.
  • Section B ranking uses Lominger competency names and definitions.
  • Step 3 of the project explicitly maps extracted competencies to Lominger for standardisation.
📌 Key Reference
Lombardo, M. M., & Eichinger, R. W. (2000). FYI: For Your Improvement – A Guide for Development and Coaching. Lominger International. (5th ed., 2011, Korn Ferry).

Meta‑analytic evidence: Korn Ferry (2019) validation study (N = 12,000+ leaders) showed Lominger competencies explain 42% of the variance in leadership effectiveness ratings.

4. Framework 3: SHL Great Eight / Universal Competency Framework Bartram (2005)

📖 Academic Origin

Source: Bartram, D. (2005). The Great Eight competencies: A criterion‑centric approach to validation. Journal of Applied Psychology, 90(6), 1185‑1203.

Core Premise: Bartram conducted a large‑scale meta‑analysis of competency models across multiple organisations and job families. He identified eight universal competency clusters (the “Great Eight”) that consistently predict job performance across roles, industries, and countries:

  • Leading & Deciding
  • Supporting & Cooperating
  • Interacting & Presenting
  • Analysing & Interpreting
  • Creating & Conceptualising
  • Organising & Executing
  • Adapting & Coping
  • Enterprising & Performing

Empirical Evidence: Bartram’s meta‑analysis (N = 4,858 individuals across 99 jobs) found that the Great Eight competencies collectively explain 62% of the variance in job performance ratings. The average corrected correlation across the eight competencies was r = 0.34 with overall performance.

Relevance to JioCX: Our 10‑competency model is a contextualised expansion of the Great Eight, tailored specifically to HR Manager and HRBP roles at JioCX. For example, we split “Organising & Executing” into Execution Excellence (EE) and Drive & Initiative (DI) to capture nuances important for our context.

🎯 How It Operates in This Survey
  • Competency selection: The 10 competencies are explicitly derived from the Great Eight clusters.
  • Item structure: Behavioural indicators (Section A) follow Bartram’s recommendation of 4‑6 items per competency.
  • Validation: We will test whether our 10‑factor model aligns with Bartram’s 8‑factor structure via exploratory factor analysis.
📌 Key Reference
Bartram, D. (2005). The Great Eight competencies: A criterion‑centric approach to validation. Journal of Applied Psychology, 90(6), 1185–1203.

Meta‑analytic evidence: Bartram (2005) found r = 0.32 (corrected for unreliability) between Great Eight competencies and overall job performance, with an R² of 0.38 in the best‑fitting regression model.

Cross‑cultural validity: Subsequent studies (e.g., Kurz & Bartram, 2002) replicated the Great Eight structure across 30+ countries, confirming its universality.

5. Framework 4: Campbell’s Performance Model Campbell (1990)

📖 Academic Origin

Source: Campbell, J. P. (1990). Modeling the performance prediction problem in industrial and organizational psychology. In M. D. Dunnette & L. M. Hough (Eds.), Handbook of Industrial and Organizational Psychology (Vol. 1, pp. 687‑732). Consulting Psychologists Press.

Core Premise: Campbell argued that job performance is multi‑dimensional, not a single unitary construct. He proposed that performance comprises three broad pillars:

  • Task Performance: Proficiency in core technical and functional job activities.
  • Contextual Performance: Behaviours that support the organisational, social, and psychological environment (e.g., cooperation, helping, organisational citizenship).
  • Adaptive Performance: Ability to handle change, learn from experience, and respond effectively to novel situations.

Empirical Evidence: Multiple meta‑analyses support Campbell’s tripartite structure:

  • Viswesvaran et al. (2005) found that task and contextual performance are distinct but correlated (ρ = 0.50–0.70).
  • Pulakos et al. (2000) demonstrated that adaptive performance is a third distinct factor, predicting performance in dynamic environments beyond task and contextual performance.
  • Schmitt & Chan (1998) reported that adaptive performance has incremental validity (ΔR² = 0.08–0.12) over task performance alone.
📊 How It Operates in This Survey
  • Task Performance: Mapped to EE, PS, DI.
  • Contextual Performance: Mapped to CI, PL, CO.
  • Adaptive Performance: Mapped to LA, RE, ST, CM.
  • This ensures the survey provides comprehensive coverage of the entire performance domain, not just task‑oriented behaviours.
📌 Key References
Campbell, J. P. (1990). Modeling the performance prediction problem. Handbook of I‑O Psychology.

Viswesvaran, C., Schmidt, F. L., & Ones, D. S. (2005). Is there a general factor in ratings of job performance? A meta‑analytic framework. Journal of Applied Psychology, 90(4), 677‑698.

Meta‑analytic evidence: Viswesvaran et al. found that task and contextual performance are distinct but correlated (ρ = 0.62), and both contribute uniquely to overall performance ratings.

6. Framework 5: Classical Test Theory Cronbach (1951) · Nunnally (1978)

📖 Academic Origin

Key Contributors:

  • Cronbach, L. J. (1951). Coefficient alpha and the internal structure of tests. Psychometrika, 16(3), 297‑334.
  • Nunnally, J. C. (1978). Psychometric Theory (2nd ed.). McGraw‑Hill.

Core Premise: CTT provides the mathematical and statistical foundation for reliability (consistency of measurement) and validity (accuracy of measurement). The core equation is:

X = T + E

where X = observed score, T = true score, and E = measurement error. The goal of survey design is to minimise E and maximise the proportion of variance attributable to T.

Key CTT Concepts Applied to This Survey:

  • Cronbach’s α: Measures internal consistency reliability (will be calculated post‑data collection).
  • Item‑total correlation: Identifies weak items that do not correlate with the overall competency score.
  • Split‑half reliability: Used to estimate the reliability of the ranking and scenario sections.
📊 How CTT Operates in This Survey
  • Reverse‑scored items: 50% (10 of 20) are reverse‑scored to reduce measurement error from acquiescence bias.
  • Multiple items per competency: 2 items per competency (increased to 4 in Survey‑2) to boost reliability (α).
  • Attention checks: Used to detect and exclude respondents with high measurement error (careless responding).
  • Data cleaning: Straight‑liner flag (SD < 0.5) identifies respondents whose responses have low true‑score variance.
📌 Key References
Cronbach, L. J. (1951). Coefficient alpha and the internal structure of tests. Psychometrika, 16(3), 297‑334.

Nunnally, J. C. (1978). Psychometric Theory (2nd ed.). McGraw‑Hill.

Acceptable α thresholds: Nunnally (1978) recommends α > 0.70 for basic research and α > 0.80 for applied decision‑making. Our target: α > 0.70 for each competency.

7. Framework Synthesis Integration Model

🔗 The Five Frameworks Work Together as a Cohesive System:
  • Iceberg provides the why – it explains that the deepest drivers of performance are hidden (motives, traits).
  • Lominger provides the what – it gives us the 67‑competency library and behavioural anchors to draw from.
  • SHL Great Eight provides the how – it shows which competencies universally predict performance across roles.
  • Campbell provides the coverage – it ensures we measure Task, Contextual, and Adaptive performance, leaving no blind spots.
  • CTT provides the rigour – it gives us the statistical tools to ensure our measurements are reliable and valid.

10. Why These Frameworks? Reinforced Justification

✅ Summary of Inclusion Rationale
  • Iceberg – Essential for capturing the hidden drivers of performance (motives, traits) that are invisible to traditional skills‑based assessments.
  • Lominger – Industry‑standard competency library; enables benchmarking against external norms and facilitates stakeholder buy‑in.
  • SHL Great Eight – Empirically validated universal competency framework; ensures the model is generalisable beyond JioCX.
  • Campbell – Ensures comprehensive performance coverage (Task + Contextual + Adaptive); prevents over‑focusing on technical skills.
  • CTT – Provides the psychometric rigour needed for reliable and valid measurement; guides item design and data cleaning.
⚠️ Why Not Other Frameworks (Brief Recap)
  • Big Five / OCEAN: Too trait‑based; weak predictive validity for specific job behaviours (r = 0.10–0.25).
  • MBTI: Lacks predictive validity; categorises rather than measures continuously.
  • DiSC: Focuses on communication styles, not performance competencies.
  • Hogan: Proprietary, expensive, and focuses on personality rather than observable competencies.
  • McKinsey 7S / Balanced Scorecard: Macro‑organisational strategy tools; not designed for individual‑level performance prediction.
  • Gallup CliftonStrengths: Measures innate talents, not applied behaviours in the workplace.

Conduct exploratory factor analysis to test whether our 10‑competency structure aligns with Bartram’s 8‑factor Great Eight structure. This will validate our framework choice.

For Survey‑2 (Phase 2), include a short 5‑item social desirability scale (e.g., from Reynolds, 1982) to control for faking and increase discriminant validity.
