✍️ Section D — Personal Reflection 2 Open-Ended Questions · Level-Adapted FIX 5: Time anchor added — forces real events, not aspirations Most important section for success profiling · 30-word minimum
👤 Junior / Executive
⚡ Why time anchors matter: Without "when did this happen?", respondents write aspirational stories, not real events. The time anchor forces behavioral evidence from actual recent experience.
Answer both questions in your own words. Write at least 30 words. You must mention a specific real event from the last 12 months — not a general statement about how you work.
D1 · Achievement & Impact
Tell us about one specific piece of work from the last 12 months that you are genuinely proud of. What exactly did YOU do, and what changed because of it?
Before you write: Identify the specific event first. Approximately when did it happen? (You don't need to write this — just use it to anchor your answer to a real memory, not a general pattern.)
📝 Open text · Min 30 words · Focus: YOUR specific action + the measurable or visible result
D2 · Growth Edge
Name one skill or behavior you know you need to improve. Then describe one specific thing you have actually done in the last 3 months to work on it — not what you plan to do.
Key: We are asking what you have already done — not what you intend to do. If you haven't done anything specific yet, be honest about that.
📝 Open text · Min 30 words · Focus: Specific development action already taken + what changed
👥 Manager / Team Lead
⚡ Bias reduction: Manager questions ask for team or individual impact — not just personal feelings. "What changed for your team or colleague" forces external evidence, not self-evaluation.
Answer both questions in your own words. Write at least 30 words. Describe specific real events from the last 12 months — not general statements about your management philosophy.
D1 · Leadership Impact
Think of a specific moment in the last 12 months when something you did as a manager made a real, positive difference for a direct report or your team. What exactly did you do — and what changed for them as a result?
Before you write: Picture the specific person and situation. Was it a conversation, a decision, a piece of feedback, removing a blocker? Start there — then describe what you did and what changed for them.
📝 Open text · Min 30 words · Focus: YOUR specific action as a manager + the change it created in another person or in the team
D2 · Leadership Growth Edge
Name one specific area where your management style needs to improve — something you've received feedback on, noticed yourself, or know is holding you or your team back. What have you actually done in the last 3 months to work on it?
Key: Honest self-assessment of a real gap — not a polished "area for growth" answer. The more specific and candid you are, the more useful this is for your own development.
📝 Open text · Min 30 words · Focus: Specific gap + specific action taken + what changed (even if the change is small)
🏛️ Leader / VP+
⚡ Bias reduction: Leader questions focus on decisions and their organizational consequences — not on personal leadership traits. "What did you decide, and what happened?" is harder to inflate than "I am a strategic leader."
Answer both questions in your own words. Write at least 30 words. Anchor to specific real decisions or initiatives from the last 12 months — not general leadership philosophy statements.
D1 · Organizational Decision
Describe one significant decision or initiative you led in the last 12 months that had impact beyond your immediate team. What exactly did you decide or do — and what was the measurable or observable outcome for the organization?
Before you write: Identify the specific decision. What were the options you considered? What did you choose? What happened? Be concrete — avoid general statements about vision or strategy.
📝 Open text · Min 30 words · Focus: The actual decision + org-level impact + what you would do differently if you had to do it again
D2 · Leadership Adaptation
Describe one specific way your leadership approach has changed in the last 12 months. What situation or feedback drove the change — and what have you actually done differently as a result?
Key: We are asking about a real, observable change in what you do — not a change in your thinking or beliefs. "I now do X instead of Y" is the kind of answer that is most useful here.
📝 Open text · Min 30 words · Focus: Specific trigger → specific behavioral change → observable result

3. How to Build the Success Profile

Step 1: Segment respondents
Split all responses into 3 groups using Manager Nomination (E3): Top (T), Average (A), Development (D). If E3 is unavailable, use E1 relative self-rating as a proxy. Exclude N (new employees).
Step 2: Compare Section A by group
Calculate mean Section A score per competency for T vs A groups. Competencies where Top performers score ≥0.5 points higher than Average = SUCCESS DIFFERENTIATORS. These go into the success profile.
Step 3: Analyze Section B rankings
Look at which competencies Top performers rank in their Top 3 — vs. what Average performers rank. This reveals what high performers believe matters (their mental model of success), not just what they do.
Step 4: Code Section C follow-ups
For each scenario's "Why?" follow-up, look at which rationale Top performers chose vs Average. This reveals the underlying VALUE SYSTEM driving behavior — the deepest layer of the success profile.
Step 5: Qualitatively code Section D
Read D responses and tag each with: (a) competency it demonstrates, (b) whether it shows internal attribution ("I did X") vs external ("my team did X"), (c) specificity (real event vs vague statement). Top performers will show more internal attribution and specificity.
Step 6: Validate against performance data
Join to (G) Employee Rating Analysis.xlsx by Employee ID. Run correlation between Section A competency scores and external performance ratings. r > 0.3 for a competency = validated predictor. Include in success profile.
