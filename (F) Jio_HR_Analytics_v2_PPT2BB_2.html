Step 1 of 4 — Data Preparation
Rating Encoding — Making Text Computable
Performance ratings are stored as text (A*, A+, A, B). We convert them to numbers so we can do maths with them.
Encoding Map
Rating Numeric Meaning
A*4Exceptional
A+3Exceeds expectations
A2Meets expectations
B1Below expectations
Why We Need This
Text strings like "A*" cannot be averaged, trended, or scored.

Once converted to numbers, we can:
✅ Calculate 5-year average
✅ Detect improvement / decline over time
✅ Measure consistency (standard deviation)
✅ Apply recency weights (recent years matter more)

These numeric columns are named RTG21-22_num through RTG25-26_num
Step 2 of 4 — Feature Engineering
4 Derived Metrics We Created
Raw ratings alone don't tell the full story. We engineered 4 new variables that capture level, direction, stability, and recency.
1
avg_rating — Overall Performance Level
Simple mean across all available rating years (RTG21-22 through RTG25-26). Missing years are excluded from the calculation (not treated as zero).
avg_rating = mean(RTG21-22_num, RTG22-23_num, ..., RTG25-26_num)
→ Proxy for: sustained performance quality across tenure
2
rating_trend — Trajectory Direction
Most recent rating minus earliest available rating. Positive = improving. Negative = declining. Zero = stable.
rating_trend = RTG25-26_num − RTG21-22_num
→ Proxy for: growth potential and career trajectory direction
3
rating_consistency — Reliability Score
Standard deviation of all year ratings. Low std dev = same level every year (reliable). High std dev = volatile year-to-year performance.
rating_consistency = std_dev(RTG21-22_num … RTG25-26_num)
→ Proxy for: behavioural reliability — key success profile criterion
4
recency_weighted_rtg — Recent-Weighted Average
Weighted average that gives more importance to recent years. A person who improved recently scores better than one who peaked 3 years ago.
= (RTG21-22 × 10%) + (RTG22-23 × 15%) + (RTG23-24 × 20%) + (RTG24-25 × 25%) + (RTG25-26 × 30%)
→ Proxy for: current performance state — most predictive of near-future performance
Step 2 of 4 — Feature Engineering
Performance Trajectory Classification
Each employee is assigned one of 6 trajectory labels based on a decision-tree logic using avg_rating, rating_trend, and rating_consistency together.
Classification Logic
if avg ≥ 3.5 AND consistency ≤ 0.5:
  → Consistent Star
elif trend ≥ 1 AND avg ≥ 2.8:
  → Rising Performer
elif trend ≤ -1 AND avg < 2.5:
  → Declining Risk
elif avg ≥ 3.0:
  → Solid Performer
else:
  → Developing

Step 3 of 4 — Normalisation
Why We Normalise to 0–100
7 variables have different natural ranges. Without normalisation, experience (0–35 years) would dominate the score just because it's a bigger number.
❌ Without Normalisation
An employee with 30 years experience + avg rating 2.5 would score higher than a 5-year employee with all A* ratings.

Reason: raw experience numbers are 10× bigger than rating numbers.
✅ With Normalisation
Every variable contributes exactly its assigned weight to the final score — no variable dominates just because of its measurement scale.

Formula: (Value − Min) / (Max − Min) × 100
7 Normalised Score Variables Created
score_avg_rating Direct normalisation of avg_rating
score_recency_rtg Direct normalisation of recency-weighted rating
score_trend Trend shifted by +2, then normalised
score_exp Reliance experience normalised
score_consistency Inverted then normalised (low std = high score)
score_promotion Binary × 100 (no normalisation needed)
score_progression Binary × 100 (no normalisation needed)
⚠️ Consistency is inverted because std deviation measures volatility — lower is better. We flip it with 1 − consistency before normalising.

The Final Formula
Composite Score — Full Calculation
One formula. One number. 0 to 100. Ranks every one of the 920 employees on the same objective scale.
Composite Score Formula
composite_score =
  (score_avg_rating × 0.30) +
  (score_recency_rtg × 0.20) +
  (score_trend × 0.15) +
  (score_consistency × 0.15) +
  (score_exp × 0.10) +
  (score_promotion × 0.05) +
  (score_progression × 0.05)

Key Findings
10 Data Inferences That Drive the Project
What the OLP data actually tells us — and what each finding means for the predictor model.
1
Grade–Performance Link (r > 0.7)
Higher grade correlates strongly with higher avg rating.
→ Grade / seniority is a top predictor; benchmarking must be grade-segmented
2
Experience is a Moderate Predictor (r = 0.417)
Total experience vs avg rating shows moderate positive correlation.
→ Include at ~10% weight; don't over-weight or it penalises newer high-performers
3
Performance Peak: Age 30–50
Avg rating: 3.0–3.19 for age 30–50 vs 2.49 for age 20–30.
→ Success profile targets mid-career professionals; add context for younger talent pipeline
4
Cohort Differentiation
JioCX and IT Infrastructure cohorts outperform others consistently.
→ Benchmark HRBP success profiles cohort-specifically — not one-size-fits-all
5
Rating Trend Identifies Rising Stars & Flight Risks
101 employees improved by 1+ grade; 100 declined over 5 years.
→ Trend variable surfaces hidden talent and early warning signals simultaneously
6
Promotion Decisions Align With Performance Data
was_promoted correlates with higher avg ratings — organisational decisions are validated.
→ Promotion flag is a reliable confirmatory signal; use as 5% weight in composite
7
Star Performers Are Consistent
Top 20% show significantly lower rating_consistency std dev than bottom 80%.
→ Reliability is a key success criterion — not just peak performance in one year
8
Recency-Weighted Rating > Simple Average
Recent years capture current performance state better than an unweighted mean.
→ Phase 2 interviews should probe recent behaviours, not historical career narrative
9
Composite Score Enables Clean Segmentation
The 0–100 scale clearly separates the 5 tiers with meaningful score gaps.
→ Provides an objective benchmark to validate interview + survey scores against
10
Success Profile Fingerprint Identified
Top 20% = higher Reliance exp, higher progression rate, consistently A+/A*.
→ This IS the baseline. Phase 2 interviews will add the behavioural layer on top of this.
