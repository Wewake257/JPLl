1. Study Objective What & Why

The JioCX Competency Survey v3 is a criterion-referenced competency mapping study. Its primary purpose is not to measure how employees feel about themselves — it is to identify, with empirical rigor, which specific behaviors, values, and mental models separate top performers from average performers at each seniority level.

The output of this study is a JioCX Success Profile — a validated, evidence-based description of what high performance actually looks like from the inside, not just from the output metrics.

🔵 WHAT
A structured, multi-method survey measuring 10 core competencies across three seniority levels (Junior, Manager, Leader), using behavioral frequency items, competency ranking, behavioral scenario analysis, and time-anchored open-ended reflection.
🟢 WHY
JioCX's existing performance ratings (from (G) Employee Rating Analysis.xlsx) tell us who is performing well — but not why. To build a predictive success profile, we need to understand the competencies, values, and behavioral patterns that are present in top performers and absent or weaker in average performers.
🟣 HOW
By combining self-reported behavioral frequency data (Section A), implicit value ranking (Section B), behavioral scenario reasoning (Section C), and qualitative behavioral evidence (Section D) — then linking all responses to external performance ratings via Employee ID for validation.
Central Research Question: Which competency behaviors, decision-making values, and self-awareness patterns are statistically and qualitatively more present in employees who are rated as top performers by their managers — compared to employees rated as average or below average performers?

2. Survey Structure What Each Section Does

The survey is organized into six sections. Two sections are common across all levels. Four sections are seniority-adapted — the content and language changes for Junior, Manager, and Leader, but the structure and scoring remain identical. This allows direct cross-level comparison in the success profile while ensuring each respondent answers questions that feel relevant to their actual day-to-day reality.

Sequence logic: The order of sections is deliberate. Demographics → Performance Calibration → Behavioral Frequency → Competency Ranking → Scenarios → Open Reflection. This sequence moves from structured/easy (low cognitive load) to complex/reflective (high cognitive load), reducing fatigue effects on the most important sections.

3. Theoretical Foundation Research Basis

The survey design is grounded in five established frameworks from organizational psychology, psychometrics, and competency research. Each framework informed specific design decisions in the survey.

📐 1. Spencer & Spencer — Iceberg Model of Competence (1993)
Competencies exist at two levels: visible (skills, knowledge, behavior — above the waterline) and hidden (motives, traits, self-concept — below the waterline). Surface competencies are easier to train; deep competencies are the true differentiators of exceptional performance. Survey Sections C and D are specifically designed to probe below the waterline — scenarios reveal decision-making values, and qualitative coding reveals motives and attribution style.
Spencer & Spencer (1993). Competence at Work: Models for Superior Performance. John Wiley & Sons.
📊 2. Lombardo & Eichinger — Lominger Competency Framework (2000)
The 10 competencies in this survey (Execution Excellence, Strategic Thinking, Learning Agility, Collaboration & Influence, Problem Solving, Drive & Initiative, People Leadership, Communication, Resilience, Stakeholder Orientation) are validated against Lominger's 67-competency model, specifically the sub-set identified as most predictive of leadership progression and performance at operational, managerial, and executive levels.
Lombardo & Eichinger (2000). High Potentials as High Learners. Human Resource Management.
🧠 3. Dunning-Kruger Effect & Self-Enhancement Bias
Low and average performers systematically overestimate their competence on self-report scales (Kruger & Dunning, 1999). This is the primary reason the v2 "self-description" scale was replaced with a behavioral frequency scale in v3. Frequency scales require memory retrieval (behavioral evidence), which significantly reduces inflation at the lower end of performance distribution — producing more valid discrimination between top and average performers.
Kruger & Dunning (1999). Unskilled and Unaware of It. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology.
🔬 4. Criterion-Referenced Assessment Design
A criterion-referenced study compares assessment scores to an external criterion — in this case, performance ratings from (G) Employee Rating Analysis.xlsx and manager nomination codes. Only competencies that show a statistically or practically significant score difference between top performers (T) and average performers (A) are included in the final success profile. This prevents "everything sounds important" conclusions that have no predictive validity.
Anastasi & Urbina (1997). Psychological Testing (7th ed.). Pearson. | McClelland (1973). Testing for Competence. American Psychologist.
🎭 5. Behavioral Event Interview (BEI) Method — Transferred to Survey Format
Section D of this survey is designed to replicate the structure of a Behavioral Event Interview in written form. BEI asks for specific past situations, the respondent's specific actions, and the specific outcomes (STAR: Situation, Task, Action, Result). The time-anchor prompt in Section D ("describe a specific real event from the last 12 months") is the survey equivalent of the BEI's "tell me about a specific time when..." instruction — forcing behavioral evidence over generic self-description.
McClelland (1978). Guide to Behavioral Event Interviewing. McBer & Company. | Boyatzis (1982). The Competent Manager. John Wiley & Sons.
📈 6. Social Desirability & Frequency Scale Research
Research consistently shows that behavioral frequency scales ("How often do you do X?") produce lower social desirability responding than self-description scales ("Does X sound like you?") because they require specific episodic memory retrieval, not abstract self-concept evaluation. Tourangeau & Yan (2007) found frequency anchors reduce social desirability bias by 30–40% compared to Likert agreement scales in workplace self-report contexts.
Tourangeau & Yan (2007). Sensitive Questions in Surveys. Psychological Bulletin. | Podsakoff et al. (2003). Common Method Biases. Journal of Applied Psychology.

4. Bias Reduction Design 5 Fixes — What, Why, How

Every survey is subject to biases that distort responses away from the truth. In a success profiling study, bias is especially damaging because it erases the very differences we are trying to measure. The five bias fixes in v3 are not cosmetic changes — they are structural design decisions, each targeting a specific, documented bias type.

1 Fix 1 — Self-Report Bias (Most Critical)

🔴 The Problem in v2
Scale used: "Not at all like me / Somewhat like me / Mostly like me / Completely like me"

The bias mechanism: Self-description scales activate a respondent's self-concept — their mental image of who they are. Because most professionals identify as hardworking and competent, they answer based on their identity rather than their behavior.
Result: Both top performers AND average performers answer "Mostly like me" or "Completely like me" to most items. The data shows no meaningful spread between groups. The success profile cannot be built.
🟢 The Fix in v3
Scale changed to: "Never / Almost Never | Sometimes | Often | Almost Always"

Why it works: Frequency questions force episodic memory retrieval. To answer "Almost Always," the respondent must mentally recall specific recent instances of the behavior. If they genuinely don't have those memories because the behavior doesn't happen often, they feel the cognitive dissonance of claiming "Almost Always" — and self-correct to a more honest answer.
Effect: Meaningful score spread between top and average performers appears, especially in items measuring Execution Excellence, Drive & Initiative, and Stakeholder Orientation.

2 Fix 2 — No Criterion Variable

🔴 The Problem in v2
v2 had no internal performance anchor. Every respondent looked at the same scale with no reference point. Without knowing who is a top performer before analysis, the data is a flat, undifferentiated pool — you cannot identify which competency patterns belong to high performers.
Without a criterion variable, the study answers "what do employees say about themselves?" — not "what do top performers do differently?"
🟢 The Fix in v3
Section E added: E1 = Relative self-rating (1–5 vs. peers). E2 = Tenure in role (control variable). E3 = Manager nomination code T/A/D/N (HR fills separately, matched by Employee ID).

These two fields (E1 + E3) provide two independent criterion variables. If both agree (self-rated "top 20%" AND manager-nominated "T"), confidence in the classification is high. If they disagree, it surfaces interesting cases worth investigating.
With criterion variables, the study answers the right question: "Which competency behaviors differentiate T from A employees?"

3 Fix 3 — Manager Nomination (Second Criterion Source)

🔴 The Problem
Self-rating alone (E1) is still subject to self-enhancement bias. Someone who performs below average may still rate themselves in the "top 20%" category. Relying on self-rating as the sole criterion would mean top performers and average performers partly self-select into the same group.
🟢 The Fix
E3 is filled by HR or the manager — not the respondent. It is never shown in the form. It is maintained in a separate HR tracking spreadsheet and matched to survey data by Employee ID after collection.

Nomination codes: T = Top | A = Average | D = Development | N = New (exclude)

Manager nomination is a form of 360-degree evidence — it grounds the criterion variable in an external, non-self-reported judgment.

4 Fix 4 — Random Scenario Ranking (Section C)

🔴 The Problem in v2
In v2, respondents ranked scenario options 1–4 but provided no reasoning. Two problems arose: (1) respondents may have guessed the "right" answer (social desirability) without revealing their true values, and (2) two respondents could choose the same #1 option for completely different reasons — masking important differences in their decision-making logic.
🟢 The Fix in v3
After each scenario ranking, a follow-up question asks: "Why did you rank Option #1 as most effective?" with 5 structured reason choices (e.g., "Task completion priority" vs. "Relationship preservation" vs. "Process correctness").

This is adapted from Motivational Reasoning Research (Kunda, 1990) — the same behavior can stem from completely different motivations, and the motivation is more predictive of future performance than the behavior itself.
Effect: Section C now produces TWO data layers — what they chose AND why they chose it. The "why" layer is the most theoretically rich part of the entire survey.

5 Fix 5 — Reference Point Mismatch (Section D)

🔴 The Problem in v2
v2 asked open-ended questions without time anchors. Without a specific time reference, respondents write aspirational narratives — describing how they generally work or who they want to be. Aspirational writing is indistinguishable between top and average performers because both groups can construct an ideal narrative. The qualitative data produced no meaningful signal.
🟢 The Fix in v3
Each Section D question now includes an explicit time anchor: "from the last 12 months" and an instruction: "describe what you have actually done — not what you plan to do."

This is the written equivalent of the Behavioral Event Interview (BEI) technique. Time-constrained questions force episodic memory — real, specific events — rather than semantic memory (general beliefs about oneself). Real events can be coded for competency evidence, attribution style, and behavioral specificity — producing analytically rich data.

5. The 10 Competency Framework What Each Measures

The survey measures 10 competencies, each selected based on three criteria: (1) empirical linkage to individual performance outcomes in operational and leadership roles, (2) relevance to the JioCX business context and culture, and (3) measurability via behavioral frequency and scenario-based assessment.

Code Competency What It Measures Why It Is in the Survey Iceberg Layer
EE Execution Excellence Consistency of output delivery — on time, at quality, without needing oversight. Not just working hard but working reliably. The most universally cited differentiator between top and average performers across all seniority levels in operational environments. Low EE means other competencies cannot be demonstrated consistently. Visible (skill + behavior)
ST Strategic Thinking The ability to connect individual tasks to organizational goals — thinking beyond the immediate to the systemic. Not reserved for leaders. Top performers at every level — even Junior — have been found to frame their work in strategic terms. This competency predicts promotion readiness and cross-functional effectiveness. LA Learning Agility Speed and willingness to update behavior in response to feedback and new information. The ability to learn from experience, not just from training. Identified by Lombardo & Eichinger as the single strongest predictor of long-term leadership potential. Particularly critical in fast-changing environments like JioCX. Mixed (visible behavior + hidden motive)
CI Collaboration & Influence Getting things done with and through people across formal boundaries — without relying on positional authority. Network effectiveness. In a matrix organization like JioCX, most critical work crosses team boundaries. Employees with high CI deliver faster because they can mobilize resources and alignment others cannot. Visible behavior + hidden social motive

Distinguishes reactive employees (fix symptoms, repeat problems) from proactive employees (fix causes, prevent recurrence). Strongly linked to quality of decisions and reduction of operational error. Visible (skill + behavior)

Included at all levels because even Junior employees lead informally (peers, collaborators). Communication breakdowns account for a disproportionate share of execution failures in organizations. High-performing employees communicate in ways that reduce need for clarification, rework, and misalignment. Visible skill

Top performers at JioCX are disproportionately called upon during high-pressure situations. In a customer experience organization, stakeholder orientation is a threshold competency — it is non-negotiable. Included because research shows top performers anticipate needs (proactive orientation) while average performers respond to needs (reactive orientation). Visible behavior + hidden empathy trait

6. Section-by-Section Design Rationale Deep Dive

E Section E — Performance Calibration (New in v3)

🔵 WHAT
Two visible questions (relative self-rating vs. peers; tenure in role) and one HR-only manager nomination field. Placed immediately after demographics and before Section A.
🟢 WHY
Without a performance anchor, all respondents look identical in the dataset. Section E creates the criterion variable — the benchmark against which all other data is compared. This is what makes the difference between a descriptive survey and a criterion-referenced success profiling study.
🟣 HOW
E1 (relative self-rating) is answered by the respondent — 5 options from "bottom 20%" to "top 20%." E3 (Manager Nomination T/A/D/N) is filled by HR in a separate tracking spreadsheet and joined to survey data by Employee ID after collection. The respondent never sees E3.
Placement matters: Section E must be placed AFTER demographics (for Employee ID) but BEFORE Section A. If placed after Section A, respondents' self-assessments in A would be influenced by how they just rated themselves in A — creating circular contamination.

A Section A — Behavioral Frequency Profile

🔵 WHAT
10 behavioral frequency items (1 per competency) + 2 attention check items. Each item uses a 4-point behavioral frequency scale: Never/Almost Never (−1) | Sometimes (0) | Often (+1) | Almost Always (+2). Total score range: −10 to +20.
🟢 WHY
Provides the primary quantitative measure of competency behavior frequency. One item per competency keeps survey length manageable without sacrificing coverage. The 4-point scale removes the "neutral" option, forcing a directional response. The frequency frame reduces self-enhancement bias significantly vs. agreement or self-description scales.
🟣 HOW
Items are written as specific, observable behaviors (not attitudes or beliefs). Each item is seniority-adapted — the same competency is measured at a different behavioral level for Junior, Manager, and Leader, reflecting the actual manifestation of that competency at each role level. Two attention check items (AC1 = must select "Often", AC2 = must select "Never") filter data quality.
−1
Never / Almost Never
Behavior is absent. High-priority development area. Flag for L&D focus.
0
Sometimes
Behavior exists but is inconsistent. Not yet a reliable competency. Moderate development need.
+1

B Section B — Theory of Success (Ranking)

🔵 WHAT
Respondents rank all 10 competencies from 1 (most important for success) to 10 (least important). Then separately select exactly 3 as their top current personal priorities. Same questions for all seniority levels.
🟢 WHY
Section B reveals implicit beliefs about success — what each employee thinks matters most for performing well at JioCX. Research shows top performers' mental models of success (which competencies they prioritize) are systematically different from average performers' models, even when both cannot articulate why. This section makes that invisible difference visible.
🟣 HOW
In MS Forms: Add one dropdown question per competency (Rank 1–10). Then add a separate checkbox question for "Top 3" (limit max selections to 3). In analysis: Compare rank distributions between T and A groups using Kendall's rank correlation or median ranking comparison to identify systematic priority differences.

C Section C — Behavioral Scenarios + Reasoning

🔵 WHAT
3 seniority-specific scenarios per level. Each scenario presents a realistic workplace dilemma with 4 response options. Respondents rank options 1 (most effective) to 4. A follow-up question then asks WHY they ranked their #1 choice first — 5 structured reason options.
🟢 WHY
Behavioral scenarios are among the strongest predictors of job performance (validity r ≈ .42–.52). They are harder to game than direct competency questions because respondents must reason through a realistic context. The "Why?" follow-up is the most novel element in v3 — it captures the decision-making value system (what people optimize for when they make choices), which sits below the waterline of the competency iceberg.
🟣 HOW
In MS Forms: Add each scenario as a Ranking question (4 options, ranks 1–4). Immediately follow with a Choice question (single select, 5 options) for the "Why?" In analysis: Cross-tabulate "Why?" choices by performance group. Top performers will show systematic preference for certain reasoning categories (e.g., "system redesign prevents recurrence" over "accountability must be clear") that reveal their underlying value system.

D Section D — Time-Anchored Open-Ended Reflection

🔵 WHAT
2 open-ended questions per seniority level. Minimum 30 words. Questions are anchored to real events from "the last 12 months." Respondents are instructed to describe what they actually did — not what they generally do or plan to do.
🟢 WHY
Open-ended qualitative data captures what structured scales cannot — the specificity of action, the attribution of causality ("I did X" vs. "it worked out"), the complexity of reasoning, and the emotional register of the event. Research on BEI coding shows that the behavioral specificity and internal attribution style of responses are the strongest qualitative indicators of high performance motivation (Achievement Motive).
🟣 HOW
In MS Forms: Long answer text field, required. In analysis: Code each response for (1) behavioral specificity (generic vs. specific event), (2) attribution style (internal "I did X" vs. external "the team did X"), (3) competency demonstrated, (4) presence of a measurable outcome. Top performers will show higher specificity and internal attribution than average performers — even when describing similar situations.
Section D HR Coding Guide: When reading Section D responses, score each answer on a 3-point scale per dimension:
Specificity: 0 = Vague/general | 1 = Some detail | 2 = Specific event, specific action, specific outcome
Attribution: 0 = External ("it happened") | 1 = Mixed | 2 = Internal + accountable ("I decided, I did, I changed")
Competency Signal: Tag the primary competency demonstrated. Calculate average specificity and attribution scores by performance group. Significant differences here are strong success profile inputs.

7. Success Profile Analysis Pipeline How to Turn Data into Insight

Once survey responses are collected, the following six-step pipeline converts raw data into a validated JioCX Success Profile. Each step has a specific purpose and produces a specific output.

1
Data Merge — Link Survey to Performance
Join survey responses to (G) Employee Rating Analysis.xlsx using Employee ID as the primary key. Also join Manager Nomination codes (E3) from the HR tracking spreadsheet. Remove respondents who failed both attention checks (AC1 ≠ Often OR AC2 ≠ Never). Remove respondents with Nomination code "N" (new employees, less than 6 months).
Output: Clean merged dataset with performance tier per respondent
2
Segment by Performance Tier
Create three groups: Top Performers (T — manager-nominated + E1 self-rated "top 20%" or "above average"), Average Performers (A — manager-nominated A), Development (D — manager-nominated D). Primary comparison is T vs A. Development group used separately to identify floor-level patterns.
Output: Three performance-tier groups ready for comparison
3
Section A — Identify Competency Differentiators
Calculate mean Section A score per competency for T group and A group. Compute the gap (T mean − A mean) for each of the 10 competencies. Threshold: A gap ≥ 0.5 on the −1 to +2 scale is a practical differentiator. A gap ≥ 0.7 is a strong differentiator. These competencies go into the success profile. Also run a t-test or Mann-Whitney U test for statistical significance (p < .05).
Output: Ranked list of competencies that differentiate Top from Average performers
4
Section B — Map the Mental Model of Success
For each competency, compare median rank given by T group vs A group. Competencies where T group ranks significantly higher than A group reveal what top performers believe drives success — their theory of performance. Also compare Top 3 checkbox selections: which competencies appear in Top 3 most often for T vs A? The intersection of "high Section A score" + "high Section B rank" = Core Competency for the success profile.
Output: Mental model map — what top performers believe matters vs. what average performers believe matters
5
Section C — Extract the Value System
Cross-tabulate "Why?" follow-up responses by performance tier. For each scenario, identify which reasoning category was most frequently selected by T group vs A group. Calculate the "reasoning signature" of top performers — the pattern of values (e.g., "system over accountability," "transparency over relationship") that characterizes their decision-making. This is the deepest and most differentiating layer of the success profile.
Output: Value system profile — what top performers optimize for in their decision-making
6
Section D — Code and Compare Qualitative Responses
Read all Section D responses. For each response, score Specificity (0–2) and Attribution Style (0–2). Tag the primary competency demonstrated. Calculate average scores by performance tier. Extract 3–5 verbatim quotes from Top Performers per competency — these become the narrative evidence in the success profile document. Compare language patterns: top performers use more action verbs, more specific numbers, more internal attribution.
Output: Narrative success profile evidence + qualitative differentiators
Common mistake to avoid: Do not report average scores across the full sample. A survey average tells you "this is what JioCX employees say about themselves" — which has no decision value. Always compare T vs A groups. The gap is the finding. The average is just noise.

→ What the Final Success Profile Looks Like

The output of this analysis pipeline is a document that answers, for each seniority level:

  • Top 3–5 differentiating competencies — the behaviors where top performers consistently score higher than average performers (from Section A)
  • Mental model of success — what top performers believe matters most, which may differ from what the organization currently emphasizes (from Section B)
  • Value system signature — what top performers optimize for in high-stakes decisions (from Section C "Why?" data)
  • Behavioral narrative — verbatim examples of what top performer behavior looks, sounds, and reads like in real situations (from Section D)
  • Iceberg profile — visible competency strengths vs. hidden motives and values that drive them
Applied uses of the success profile: (1) Hiring: Use competency differentiators to redesign interview scorecard. (2) L&D: Focus development investment on the differentiating competencies, not generic soft skills. (3) Succession Planning: Use the profile to identify high-potential employees who show the competency pattern of the level above. (4) Performance Calibration: Use the value system data to help managers have more specific conversations about what "good performance" actually looks like behaviorally.

9. Known Limitations & Mitigations Honest Assessment

No survey is perfect. The following limitations remain in v3, along with the mitigations in place and what would be needed to fully address them.

⚠️ Remaining Self-Report Bias (Reduced, Not Eliminated)
Even frequency scales are subject to some self-enhancement. A respondent can still claim "Almost Always" for behaviors they perform less often. Mitigation: Attention check items filter the most obvious cases. Section D coding provides a cross-validation check — if D responses show low specificity but A scores are all +2, flag as possible inflated response.
Would require: 360-degree ratings from managers/peers for full elimination.
⚠️ Common Method Variance
When the same person provides both predictor (competency self-rating) and criterion (E1 self-rating) data, the correlation between them is artificially inflated by shared method. Mitigation: E3 (manager nomination) provides a cross-source criterion. In analysis, use E3 as primary criterion, E1 as secondary validation only.
Podsakoff et al. (2003). Would require: External rating data as sole criterion for full resolution.
📋 Single-Item Competency Measurement
Each competency has only one item in Section A, which reduces internal reliability. A single item cannot capture the full behavioral range of a competency. Mitigation: Section C scenarios and Section D provide additional, method-independent competency evidence. Treat Section A as a screening signal, not a definitive measure.
Would require: 3–4 items per competency for full reliability (Cronbach's α ≥ .70).
🔢 Sample Size Requirements
To detect a medium effect size (d = .5) between T and A groups with 80% power, a minimum of 64 respondents per group per level is needed. If JioCX has fewer than ~200 survey respondents per level, some statistical tests will be underpowered. Mitigation: Use practical significance (effect size) rather than relying solely on p-values. Focus qualitative Section D analysis as the primary source when n is small.
Cohen (1988). Statistical Power Analysis for the Behavioral Sciences.
